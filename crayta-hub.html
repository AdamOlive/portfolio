Crayta - Hub

The Hub is the ever-changing heart of Crayta, and a large part of my role has been to update it with seasonal content and minigames, as well as overseeing a major design refresh to ensure it was as effective as possible at grabbing players' attention, and telling them what Crayta's all about.

Hub Redesign

For a couple of years, Crayta had a largely white, wide-open, abstract hub, but more and more I felt that this wasn't showing Crayta to its full artistic potential. Additionally, it was difficult for the artists to apply seasonal theming, and for me to add minigames in a consistent place, and it didn't facilitate social interaction as well as it could.

I researched other central spaces in social games and UGC platforms, as well as looking at physical "hubs" such as airports and train stations. I came up with 3 measurable objectives for the new Hub that I hypothesised would improve overall retention in Crayta, particularly for new users. Working with the art team, I created and iterated on a new hub design, including a new aesthetic and layout, to meet my 3 objectives. The objectives, in short, were to give a powerful impression of Crayta as a high fidelity creative platform, to connect players with the best UGC experiences, and to facilitate social interactions.

Tabletop Champs Hub

For this most recent hub update, I opted to build a set of board games that would provide a unique experience for players that hadn't been done in Crayta before, as well as providing these as packages so that other creators could easily enhance their own social spaces. Additionally, they showcase the new dragging input method for touch devices.

The games include Tic Tac Toe, Checkers and Chess. I started with Tac Tac Toe as the lowest complexity game, to build, iterate and polish the game loop and drag mechanics, before diving into the more complex validation checks for Checkers, and finally Chess, which includes full checks for valid checkmate, castling and en passant.

This last hub has seen the highest average session lengths as a proportion of overall session lengths in Crayta.

Harrowing High Hub

The Harrowing High art content was truly inspiring, particularly the monster avatar outfits, and I wanted to create a hub that showcased one of these as an NPC to inspire creators to use the outfits in their own creations.

This hub update had a particularly ambitious game, in which players could enter a school and see how long they could survive for. An AI monster would hunt the players with an A* pathfinding algorithm, built as a Lua package that I had also provided to community creators.

Cybotanica Hub

With development time being extremely short for Cybotanica, I opted to try a new approach with the Cybotanica Hub, using it to showcase community creativity rather, than creating a minigame as most of the other hubs had.

The Cybotanica Hub had a "sculpture garden" area that showcased 3 sculptures, which were scripted to rotate weekly. They were initially populated by 3 of my sculptures, which included a miniature city, a robot NPC "charging up", and a dancing robot made up of retro parts such as an old PC, which were attached to different sockets on a hidden NPC to create the effect of a character built from junk.

Cursed Galleons Hub

The Cursed Galleons season spanned across the major Hub redesign, and therefore had to fit both designs seamlessly. This season was also full of pirate assets, but critically we didn't have a full pirate ship yet - something that would be technically as well as artistically challenging to build using Crayta's voxel + mesh system.

I decided to build a hidden object game, with daily randomised object positions, set in a pirate ship. I also built the ship, from a number of carefully rotated voxel pieces giving the appearance of rounded sides and smooth curves. Being contained within the ship, the game could easily be transferred between hub layouts. I released the ship as a community package, and it saw use in a wide variety of UGC games.

Strength and Sorcery Hub

The Strength and Sorcery season's assets focused on two contrasting themes - the "strength" side, including knights and castles, and the "sorcery" side, including potions and magical woodland elements.

To complement the season's assets, I designed a hub with each side being one of those themes, and a team-based asynchronous crafting battle game that took place between them. Each week, players could join one of the team's, and find ingredients hidden around the Hub to craft projectiles, which were shot from a cannon at the other team's side. Damage was stored as a global counter, and was reflected by environmental changes, with the buildings becoming more damaged over time.
